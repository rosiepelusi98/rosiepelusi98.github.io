The Gestalt Principles in Design

An introduction to the Laws of Perception

What is Gestalt psychology?

Gestalt principles, or laws of perception, are a framework for understanding human perception and tendency for pattern seeking (Bustamante, N 2023). These laws see use across different disciplines such as psychology, education, and visual design. Max Wertheimer is considered the founder of this school of psychology when he published his paper “Experimentelle Studien über das Sehen von Bewegung” (“Experimental Studies of the Perception of Movement”) in 1912 (Encyclopaedia Brittanica 2024).

What are the principles?

The Gestalt principles have seen some change since they were first introduced in the early 20th century and may vary depending on the discipline applying them. In the case of design Nielsen Norman Group suggest that similarity, continuation, closure, proximity, common region, figure/ground, and symmetry apply best (Gordon, K2020). Their reasoning can be found in their article Principles of Visual Design in UX.

Similarity – Elements that share characteristics are perceived as grouped.

Continuation – The human eye prefers to follow a flow of a line and will ignore any breaks in that line.

Closure – The brain prefers to see complete shapes and will “fill in the gaps” where necessary.

Proximity – Elements that are closer together are grouped together.

Common Region – Elements in an enclosed area are seen as part of a group even if they share characteristics with other elements.

Figure/Ground – An element that contrasts its background draws the eye first.

Symmetry – Humans prefer to see symmetry and can differentiate elements better when laid out in a grid like pattern.

Law of Similarity in Action

Expanding on the previous description, the law of similarity is utilised by designers by giving elements they wish the user to see as a group similar traits. These traits can be anything from colour or shape to font or orientation (Moran, K 2024). This principle is especially useful for when elements need to be singled out from a large list or grid. The singling out application can been seen in effect when looking at a link on a page. By giving a link a different colour and underlining it a designer can communicate that this particular text is different to other text on the page.
